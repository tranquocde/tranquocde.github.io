From concept to Data analysis
3 frameworks:
  • Classical : equally likely
    • The classical framework is used to calculate probabilities for experiments where each outcome is equally likely, such as flipping a coin or rolling a die. In these cases, the probability of an event is simply the number of favorable outcomes divided by the total number of possible outcomes.
    • For example, if we flip a fair coin, there are two equally likely outcomes: heads or tails. The probability of getting heads is 1/2, and the probability of getting tails is also 1/2.
    • Similarly, if we roll a fair six-sided die, there are six equally likely outcomes: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, or 6. The probability of rolling a 3, for example, is 1/6, and the probability of rolling an even number is 3/6 (or 1/2).
  • Frequentist : relative frequency
    • It assumes that probability is a measure of the long-term relative frequency of an event, and that the probability of an event can be estimated by conducting a large number of independent trials and counting the number of times the event occurs.
    • For example, if we flip a fair coin 1000 times and get 500 heads and 500 tails, we might estimate the probability of getting heads as 0.5. If we repeat this experiment many times and calculate the proportion of heads in each trial, we would expect this proportion to converge to 0.5 as the number of trials approaches infinity.
  • Bayesian : personal perspective
    • The key idea of the Bayesian framework is that probability is a measure of uncertainty or degree of belief, and that it can be updated as new information becomes available. By incorporating prior knowledge and assumptions into the model, the Bayesian approach can provide more accurate and nuanced predictions compared to traditional frequentist methods.
    • Example of Bayesian framework :
    You consider results from past elections, factoring in demographic changes as well as your colleague’s appeal in your community before concluding that she has a 3/4 chance of winning an upcoming election.
Difference between Bayesian and Frequentist:
For example, suppose you are trying to estimate the probability of a patient having a certain disease based on their symptoms. In the frequentist framework, you might collect a large sample of patients with and without the disease and calculate the proportion of patients with the disease who have the symptoms. In contrast, in the Bayesian framework, you would start with a prior belief about the probability based on your prior knowledge or assumptions. As you observe more patients with and without the disease and their symptoms, you can update your belief using Bayes' theorem to obtain a revised probability estimate.

Frequentist inference:
  • Definition:
    • Frequentist inference is based on the idea that the data used for inference is a random sample from a population, and that the properties of the sample can be used to make inferences about the population.
    • In frequentist inference, the focus is on the frequency or proportion of times that a particular event or outcome occurs. For example, if we are interested in estimating the mean height of a population of people, we might take a random sample of individuals from that population and use the sample mean as an estimate of the population mean. We would also use statistical methods to calculate a confidence intervalto estimate the range of values within which the population mean is likely to fall.
    • Frequentist inference typically involves hypothesis testing, where a null hypothesis is tested against an alternative hypothesis. The null hypothesis is a statement about the population parameter of interest(e.g., the mean, proportion, or variance), while the alternative hypothesis is a statement that contradicts the null hypothesis. The goal is to determine whether the data provide enough evidence to reject the null hypothesis in favor of the alternative hypothesis.
Under the frequentist paradigm, you view the data as a random sample from some larger, potentially hypothetical population. We can then make probability statements i.e, long-run frequency statements based on this larger population
  • Example:
    • An example of frequentist inference is a hypothesis test to determine whether a coin is fair or biased towards heads or tails. Suppose we have a coin that we suspect may be biased towards heads. To test this hypothesis, we flip the coin 100 times and observe that it comes up heads 60 times and tails 40 times.
    • In frequentist inference, we would start by formulating a null hypothesis that the coin is fair (i.e., has a 50-50 chance of coming up heads or tails) and an alternative hypothesis that the coin is biased towards heads. We would then use statistical methods to calculate the probability of observing a result as extreme or more extreme than the one we observed, assuming that the null hypothesis is true. This probability is known as the p-value.
    • If the p-value is below a certain threshold (e.g., 0.05), we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the data provide evidence that the coin is biased towards heads. If the p-value is above the threshold, we fail to reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the data do not provide enough evidence to support the alternative hypothesis.
  • Confidence interval:
    • A confidence interval is a range of values that is likely to contain the true value of a population parameter, such as the mean or proportion, based on a sample of data. It is a statistical tool used to estimate the range of possible values for a population parameter with a certain level of confidence.
    • The confidence interval is calculated from the sample data and provides an estimate of the population parameter's true value. The level of confidence indicates the probability that the true population parameter falls within the interval.
    • For example, suppose a survey was conducted to estimate the average income of a population. The sample mean income was found to be $50,000, with a standard deviation of $5,000. A 95% confidence interval for the population mean incomecould be calculated as $50,000 ± 1.96 ($5,000 / sqrt(n)), where n is the sample size. This would give a range of $48,166 to $51,834 as the likely range for the true population mean income with 95% confidence.
    • The confidence interval indicates the precision of the estimate and allows researchers to make inferences about the population based on the sample data. A wider confidence interval indicates less precision in the estimate, while a narrower confidence interval indicates greater precision. The level of confidence can be adjusted to balance the precision of the estimate with the risk of making a type I error (rejecting a true null hypothesis)
  • Likelihood function and maximum a likelihood:
    • The likelihood function is a fundamental concept in statistical inference that is used to estimate the parameters of a statistical model based on observed data. It is a function that measures how well the model fits the observed data, as a function of the model parameters.
    • The likelihood function is defined as the probability of observing the data, given a particular set of model parameters. Mathematically, it is written as: L(θ∣x)=f(x∣θ)L(\theta|x) = f(x|\theta)L(θ∣x)=f(x∣θ)﻿. where L is the likelihood function, θ is the vector of model parameters, x is the observed data, and f(x | θ) is the probability density function(PDF) or probability mass function (PMF) of the data, given the model parameters.
    • The likelihood function is often used in maximum likelihood estimation(MLE), which is a method of estimating the parameters of a statistical model by maximizing the likelihood function over the parameter space. In MLE, the parameter estimates are chosen to maximize the likelihood function, which means that they are the values of the parameters that make the observed data most likely.
    • The likelihood function provides a way to assess the goodness of fit of a model to the data, and to compare different models to each other. For example, the likelihood ratio test (LRT) compares the likelihood of two models to determine which one fits the data better.
Bayesian inference:
  • Definition:
    • Bayesian inference takes a different perspective from frequentist inference, in that it treats unknown parameters as random variables with probability distributions, rather than fixed but unknown values.
    • In Bayesian inference, we start with a prior probability distribution for the parameters of interest, which represents our beliefs or knowledge about the parameters before we observe any data. We then update this prior distribution with the observed data using Bayes' theorem, to obtain a posterior distribution of the parameters. This posterior distribution represents our updated beliefs or knowledge about the parameters, given the observed data.
  • Difference between Bayesian and frequentist inference:
    • Treatment of unknown parameters: In frequentist inference, unknown parameters are considered fixed but unknown values, while in Bayesian inference, unknown parameters are treated as random variables with probability distributions.
    • Prior beliefs: Bayesian inference allows for the incorporation of prior beliefs or knowledge about the parameters, while frequentist inference does not. In Bayesian inference, the prior distribution represents our beliefs before we observe any data, and is updated with the observed data to obtain a posterior distribution. In frequentist inference, there is no explicit representation of prior beliefs.
    • Probability statements: Bayesian inference provides probability statements directly about the parameters of interest, while frequentist inference provides probability statements about the observed data. In Bayesian inference, the posterior distributionrepresents the probability of different values of the parameters, given the observed data. In frequentist inference, the p-value represents the probability of observing a result as extreme or more extreme than the one observed, assuming the null hypothesis is true.
    • Interpretation of probability: Bayesian inference interprets probability as a measure of uncertainty or degree of belief, while frequentist inference interprets probability as the long-run frequency of an event occurring.
    • Hypothesis testing: In Bayesian inference, hypothesis testing is typically done by evaluating the posterior distribution and comparing the probabilities of different hypotheses. In frequentist inference, hypothesis testing is typically done by calculating a p-value and comparing it to a significance level.
  • Example:
    • An example of Bayesian inference is estimating the probability of a person having a disease based on their test results. Suppose that a medical test is available to detect a disease, and that the test has a false positive rate of 5% and a false negative rate of 10%. We also have prior information that the disease is rare, affecting only 1 in 1000 people in the population.
    • If a person tests positive for the disease, we can use Bayesian inference to estimate the probability that they actually have the disease. We start with a prior distribution representing our prior beliefs about the probability of a person having the disease, based on the population prevalence. In this case, the prior distribution would be a probability distribution with a mean of 0.001 (the prevalence rate) and a small variance
    • We can then update this prior distribution with the test result using Bayes' theorem, to obtain a posterior distribution of the probability that the person has the disease. The posterior distribution will depend on the person's test result, and will be shifted towards higher probabilities if the person tested positive, and towards lower probabilities if the person tested negative.
    • The posterior distribution can be used to estimate the probability that the person actually has the disease, given their test result. For example, if the person tests positive, the posterior distribution may have a mean of 0.05 (the false positive rate) for those who do not have the disease, and a mean of 0.9 (the true positive rate) for those who do have the disease. The probability that the person actually has the disease can be estimated as the proportion of the posterior distribution that is above a certain probability threshold (e.g., 0.5).
    • Bayesian inference allows for the incorporation of prior information about the disease prevalence, and provides a way to quantify the uncertainty in the estimates of the probability of having the disease. It also allows for the updating of the probability estimates with new data, such as additional test results or clinical information.
  • Posterior interval (a.k.a credible interval):
    • A posterior interval is a range of values that is calculated based on a posterior distribution, which is the distribution of values for a parameter of interest after taking into account observed data and prior beliefs. The posterior interval represents a range of plausible values for the parameter, given the observed data and prior beliefs.
    • For example, in Bayesian statistical inference, if we are trying to estimate a parameter, such as the mean of a population, we can obtain a posterior distribution for the parameter by combining a prior distribution with the likelihood function of the observed data. Once we have the posterior distribution, we can calculate a posterior interval, which is a range of values that contains a certain level of the posterior probability mass. This range is often expressed as a credible interval, which is similar in interpretation to a confidence interval in frequentist statistics.
Prior, prior predictive, posterior predictive distributions:
  • The prior predictive distribution is the distribution of observations we expect before we observe any data. It is useful for assessing whether the choice of prior distribution captures our prior beliefs.
  • In Bayesian statistics, the prior predictive distribution is a probability distribution that describes the range of possible outcomes that can be generated from a model based on the prior distribution of the model parameters. It is a way to simulate data that would be expected under the prior distribution of the parameters, before any data is observed.
  • The prior predictive distribution is obtained by first sampling values from the prior distribution of the model parameters, and then using these parameter values to generate simulated data from the model. The simulated data can then be used to explore the properties of the model and to make predictions about future data.

Poisson data:
  • The Poisson distribution is a discrete probability distribution that is used to model the number of occurrences of a rare event in a fixed interval of time or space.
  • The Poisson distribution is often used to model rare events, where the probability of occurrence is small, but the number of possible occurrences is large.
  • The Poisson distribution can be used to model a variety of phenomena, including the number of customers arriving at a store, the number of defects in a manufacturing process, and the number of mutations in a DNA sequence.
  • Example in real-world:
    • Suppose a call center receives an average of 50 calls per hour. We can model the number of calls received in a given hour using the Poisson distribution, with λ = 50. Using the probability mass function of the Poisson distribution, we can calculate the probability of different numbers of calls being received in a given hour.

      For example, we can calculate the probability of receiving exactly 60 calls in a given hour:

      P(X = 60) = (e^(-50) * 50^60) / 60!

      = 0.0097

      This means that the probability of receiving exactly 60 calls in a given hour is approximately 0.01, or 1%.

Exponential data:
  • The exponential distribution is a probability distribution that describes the time between events in a Poisson process, where events occur continuously and independently at a constant average rate. It is a continuous probability distribution that is often used to model the waiting time between events.
  • Example in real-world:
    • An example of the exponential distribution is the time between successive earthquakes. The occurrence of earthquakes can be modeled as a Poisson process, where earthquakes occur randomly and independently at a constant average rate. The time between successive earthquakes can be modeled by the exponential distribution.
    • Suppose the average rate of earthquakes in a particular region is λ = 0.1 per year. Then the probability that the time between successive earthquakes is less than or equal to t years is given by the cumulative distribution function of the exponential distribution:

      F(t; λ) = 1 - e^(-λt) For example, the probability that the time between successive earthquakes is less than or equal to 10 years is: F(10; 0.1) = 1 - e^(-0.1 * 10) = 0.6321 This means that there is a 63.21% chance that an earthquake will occur within the next 10 years.

Non-informative priors:
  • In Bayesian statistics, a prior distribution represents the degree of belief or uncertainty in a parameter before observing any data. A non-informative prior is a prior distribution that expresses minimal or no information about the parameter of interest.
  • In other words, a non-informative prior is a prior that is chosen to have a minimal impact on the posterior distribution
  • Non-informative priors are often used when the available prior information is limited or unreliable, or when the goal is to avoid introducing bias into the analysis.
  • Improper prior: Doesn’t integrate or sum to 1.
  • In simple model, non-informative priors often produce posterior mean estimates that are equivalent to the common frequentist/MLE estimates
